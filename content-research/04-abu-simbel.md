# Abu Simbel — research dossier

> Local photos: Great Temple, Nefertari (Small Temple), Amada (photo sits in aswan/nubian-village).
> Web images needed: Qasr Ibrim, Wadi el-Seboua, Kalabsha (all Wikimedia, stable).

Intro: The two famous temples were carved into a cliff by Ramesses II in the 13th century BC. In the 1960s, rising water from Lake Nasser would have drowned them, so a UNESCO team cut both into blocks and rebuilt them on higher ground. The same rescue effort saved a string of smaller Nubian temples nearby, several now in open-air sites you can visit on a Lake Nasser cruise or longer land trip.

## Attractions

### Abu Simbel Great Temple (Ramesses II)  [local photo]
Four seated statues of Ramesses II, each ~20m tall, guard the front of this temple cut straight into the rock. Inside, a hall of pillars carved as the king leads back to a sanctuary where four gods sit in the dark. Twice a year (around 22 Feb and 22 Oct) the rising sun reaches all the way in and lights three of the four statues — timing the builders worked out 3,000+ years ago. The whole temple was moved up the hillside in the 1960s, and the engineers kept the sun alignment almost exactly.
- Don't miss: stand close to the four colossi early, before the light flattens, to feel the scale against people walking past.
- Good to know: the sun-festival dates draw big crowds and higher prices — book months ahead if you want to be there for it.

### Temple of Nefertari (the Small Temple)  [local photo]
Ramesses II built this second temple next door for his wife Nefertari, dedicated to the goddess Hathor. The six statues on the front show the queen the same height as the king — very unusual for the time. Inside, the carvings are softer and more personal, with scenes of the royal couple making offerings. A short walk from its larger neighbour.
- Don't miss: the wall scenes showing Nefertari being crowned by Hathor and Isis.
- Good to know: less crowded than the Great Temple — a good place to slow down and look closely.

### Amada Temple  [local photo]
The oldest temple on Lake Nasser, started under Thutmose III and Amenhotep II in the 15th century BC. Plain outside, but the painted carvings inside are some of the best preserved in Nubia, with colour still clinging to the stone. Rather than cutting it apart, engineers put the whole temple on rails and slid it ~2.5km to higher ground in one piece.
- Don't miss: the early carved scenes inside that still hold their original paint.
- Good to know: most people reach Amada by Lake Nasser cruise rather than road — check how your trip plans to get there.

### Qasr Ibrim  [WEB IMAGE]
Different from the other sites: a ruined fortress city on a high bluff, and it was never moved. Before the lake filled it stood on a cliff far above the Nile; now the water has risen around it and left it on a small island. People lived here for thousands of years — Egyptian, Roman, Christian and Islamic — and the dry soil preserved remarkable finds, including textiles and documents. You can't land on it; you view it from the water.
- Don't miss: the outline of the Christian-era cathedral still visible among the hilltop ruins.
- Good to know: cruise-only, seen from the boat, no landing — bring a zoom lens or binoculars.
- Web image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/Qasr_Ibrim_02.jpg

### Wadi el-Seboua (Valley of the Lions)  [WEB IMAGE]
Named "valley of the lions" after the avenue of sphinxes lining the approach. Ramesses II built it; part is free-standing, the inner rooms cut into rock. An odd later twist: early Christians plastered over some of the Egyptian gods to use it as a church, and in one spot a painted saint appears to face the old carved figures behind him. Two other rescued temples, Dakka and Maharraqa, were rebuilt close by — a three-in-one stop.
- Don't miss: the row of sphinxes leading up to the entrance.
- Good to know: the three temples sit a short walk apart in open desert with little shade — go early, carry water.
- Web image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d9/Sphinx_of_Ramesses_II_from_Wadi_es-Sebua_temple_by_Dennis_G._Jarvis.jpg

### Kalabsha Temple  [WEB IMAGE]
One of the largest free-standing temples in Nubia, built ~30 BC in the early Roman period for a local sun god, Mandulis. Taken apart into ~13,000 blocks and rebuilt on an island near the Aswan High Dam, which makes it one of the easier rescued temples to reach from Aswan. The site also holds the Kiosk of Qertassi and the rock-cut Beit el-Wali temple.
- Don't miss: the well-preserved gateway and the views across the water from the temple platform.
- Good to know: reaching the island usually means a short motorboat ride — agree the price before you set off.
- Web image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1d/Kalabsha_temple.JPG

### Eskaleh Nubian Ecolodge
- Location: Abu Simbel · ~3★ guesthouse / Nubian eco-lodge · ~$60–90/night
- Description: A small mud-brick lodge run by a local Nubian owner, with a handful of rooms and a home-cooked menu. The most characterful place to stay in the village, on the lake edge a short distance from the temples.
- Amenities: lake-view rooms, traditional restaurant, garden & sun terrace, free WiFi, free parking, sunrise boat trips

### Tuya Hotel
- Location: Abu Simbel · 3★ / small Nubian-style hotel · ~$35–60/night
- Description: A friendly budget hotel about a ten-minute walk from the temples, with a few simple, clean rooms. Guests single out the breakfast, the home-style dinners and the easy walk to the monuments.
- Amenities: free breakfast, restaurant, free WiFi, free parking, A/C, free shuttle
