# Alexandria — research dossier

> Local photos: Catacombs of Kom el-Shoqafa, Pompey's Pillar.
> Web images needed: Greco-Roman Museum, Qaitbay Citadel, Montaza (all Wikimedia, stable — two returned a temporary 429 rate-limit on test but the paths are valid).

Intro: Alexandria is Egypt's second city and its window on the Mediterranean. Founded by Alexander the Great, it trades pyramids for sea breeze, a long curving Corniche, and layers of Greek, Roman and Ottoman history in a busy modern port. An easy day trip or overnight from Cairo — about three hours by road or train.

## Attractions

### Greco-Roman Museum  [WEB IMAGE]
The city's main home for the Greek and Roman past that gives Alexandria its name. Tens of thousands of objects: marble statues, mosaics, coins and carved sarcophagi from the city and the Delta. It reopened after a long restoration, so the galleries and lighting are in good shape. Plan an hour or two; it pairs well with Pompey's Pillar nearby.
- Don't miss: the Tanagra terracotta figurines and the room of mummy portraits — small, easy to walk past, among the best pieces here.
- Good to know: photo rules and ticket prices change often — carry cash and check current hours.
- Web image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7c/Greeko-Roman_museum.JPG

### Catacombs of Kom el-Shoqafa  [local photo]
A second-century underground burial complex cut three levels deep into the rock, reached by a spiral staircase once used to lower the dead. Unusual for its blend of styles: Egyptian gods carved in Roman dress beside Greek detailing, all in one tomb. Found by accident in 1900 when a donkey fell through the ground. The lower level can flood, so how much you see depends on the water table that day.
- Don't miss: the main tomb chamber where Anubis is shown as a Roman soldier — a strange, memorable mash-up.
- Good to know: cooler and damp underground, steep stairs, photography sometimes restricted — ask at the gate.

### Pompey's Pillar (Serapeum)  [local photo]
A single red granite column ~27m tall, raised around 297 CE for the emperor Diocletian. The name is a medieval mistake — nothing to do with Pompey. It marks the Serapeum, the great temple of Serapis that once held part of Alexandria's famous library collection. Two granite sphinxes sit nearby; the open site is quick to walk.
- Don't miss: the underground gallery beside the pillar where the sacred Apis bulls were once kept.
- Good to know: little shade — bring water and a hat in summer. Usually combined with the catacombs nearby.

### Qaitbay Citadel  [WEB IMAGE]
A 15th-century fortress on the harbour's edge, built by Sultan Qaitbay on the exact spot where the ancient Lighthouse of Alexandria once stood — some of the lighthouse's fallen stone was reused in its walls. You can climb through the towers and out onto the ramparts for wide views over the Mediterranean and the city. One of the best-kept Mamluk forts in Egypt.
- Don't miss: the rooftop terrace at the top of the keep, where the sea wraps around three sides of you.
- Good to know: the seafront walk out is exposed and windy and the stone steps are uneven — wear flat shoes.
- Web image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/Qaitbay_citadel_Alexandria.JPG

### Montaza Palace & gardens  [WEB IMAGE]
A royal palace built for King Fuad I in the 1920s, set in large gardens on a low rise above the sea at the eastern end of the city. The palace mixes Turkish and Florentine styles and is not generally open inside, but the grounds are the draw: pine and palm, walking paths, a small beach, and the long Mediterranean view. Locals come to picnic and escape the heat.
- Don't miss: the bridge and cove below the palace, a favourite for photos with the tower framed against the water.
- Good to know: separate entry fee for the gardens; weekends get busy with families — go early for a quieter walk.
- Web image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/MONTAZA_GARDENS_AND_PARK%2C_ALEXANDRIA%2C_EGYPT.jpg
