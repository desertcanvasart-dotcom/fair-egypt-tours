## Attractions

### Valley of the Kings  [local photo]
Burial ground of the New Kingdom pharaohs, with more than 60 tombs cut deep into the hills on the west bank of the Nile. The painted walls and ceilings still hold their colour after more than 3,000 years, covered in hieroglyphs and scenes meant to guide the dead into the afterlife. Tutankhamun's tomb was found here largely intact in 1922, which is why so many of his treasures survived. A standard ticket lets you into three tombs, and which ones are open rotates to limit wear.
- Don't miss: the tomb of Seti I if it is open — the longest and most finely decorated in the valley (separate premium ticket).
- Good to know: open ~6am–5pm. General ticket covers three tombs; Tutankhamun, Seti I and Nefertari cost extra. Budget 2–3 hours, go early.

### Karnak Temple  [local photo]
Not one temple but a vast complex built and added to by pharaohs over roughly 2,000 years, the largest religious site of the ancient world. The standout is the Great Hypostyle Hall, where 134 giant columns stand in tight rows. You will also find obelisks, a sacred lake, and an avenue of ram-headed sphinxes.
- Don't miss: the Great Hypostyle Hall early, when low light catches the carvings and crowds are thin.
- Good to know: open ~6am–5:30pm. Allow 2–3 hours. Evening sound-and-light show has a separate ticket.

### Luxor Museum  [WEB IMAGE]
A smaller, well-organised museum on the corniche overlooking the Nile — an easy contrast to the open-air sites. Displays are carefully chosen rather than crammed, with strong lighting and clear labels. Highlights include statues from the Luxor Temple cache, items from Tutankhamun's tomb, and two royal mummies.
- Don't miss: the wall of carved talatat blocks from Akhenaten's dismantled Karnak temple, reassembled into one long panel.
- Good to know: usually two sessions (~9am–2pm, then late afternoon–evening); hours shift by season. Budget 1–2 hours; tickets are inexpensive.
- Web image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4c/Luxor_Museum_2010.jpg

### Hatshepsut's Temple (Deir el-Bahari)  [local photo]
Built for one of Egypt's few female pharaohs, this mortuary temple rises in three wide terraces straight out of the cliffs. The design is unusually clean for its age, with long colonnades and ramps lined up with the rock face. Reliefs record Hatshepsut's reign, including a famous trading expedition to Punt. Close to the Valley of the Kings, so the two pair well in one morning.
- Don't miss: the Punt colonnade, with carvings of the ships, goods and people brought back.
- Good to know: open ~6am–5pm. Allow about an hour. Little shade — bring water and a hat, come early.

### Luxor Temple  [local photo]
In the centre of town, built mainly by Amenhotep III and Ramesses II, once linked to Karnak by an avenue of sphinxes nearly two miles long. A single obelisk still stands at the entrance; its twin is in Paris. Easy to reach on foot and especially worth seeing after dark, when the lighting picks out the columns and statues.
- Don't miss: the temple after sunset — lit up, cooler and quieter.
- Good to know: open until ~9pm or later — the rare site you can visit in the evening. Budget 1–2 hours; walkable from most East Bank hotels.
